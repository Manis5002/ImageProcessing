# GROUP A: 1-MARK QUESTIONS (SHORT ANSWER)

**A Simple Image Model & Geometric Model:**

**Q. 1. State the Simple Image Model equation.**
**Main Answer:** The simple image model is defined as **$f(x,y) = i(x,y) \cdot r(x,y)$**, where the image intensity at any point is the product of illumination and reflectance.

**Q. 2. In the image model $f(x,y) = i(x,y) \cdot r(x,y)$, what do $i(x,y)$ and $r(x,y)$ denote?**
**Main Answer:** Here, **$i(x,y)$** denotes the **illumination** (amount of light incident on the scene), and **$r(x,y)$** denotes the **reflectance** (amount of light reflected by the object).

**Q. 3. What is the typical range of values for the reflectance component $r(x,y)$?**
**Main Answer:** The reflectance component strictly ranges between 0 (total absorption) and 1 (total reflectance), denoted as **$0 < r(x,y) < 1$**.

**Q. 4. Write the 2D transformation matrix for the Translation of an image.**
**Main Answer:** The $3 \times 3$ homogeneous transformation matrix for translation by $(t_x, t_y)$ is:
$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Q. 5. What is the purpose of using Homogeneous Coordinates in geometric transformations?**
**Main Answer:** **Homogeneous coordinates** allow translation, scaling, and rotation to be unified under a single operation of **matrix multiplication**, enabling easy combination (compositing) of multiple transformations.

**Q. 6. Write the 2D transformation matrix for Scaling.**
**Main Answer:** The homogeneous transformation matrix for scaling by $(S_x, S_y)$ is:
$$ \begin{bmatrix} S_x & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & S_y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Q. 7. If an image is scaled by factors $S_x=1$ and $S_y=-1$, what happens to the image?**
**Main Answer:** Scaling by $S_y = -1$ causes a **reflection** of the image across the X-axis, effectively resulting in a **vertical flip**.

**Q. 8. Write the 2D transformation matrix to rotate an image by an angle $\theta$ about the origin.**
**Main Answer:** The homogeneous matrix for counter-clockwise rotation by $\theta$ is:
$$ \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Perspective Projection, Sampling & Quantization:**

**Q. 9. What is Perspective Projection in image formation?**
**Main Answer:** **Perspective projection** is the geometric transformation that maps 3D world coordinates onto a 2D image plane, where all lines of sight converge at a single point called the center of projection.

**Q. 10. Name the standard camera model used to explain perspective projection.**
**Main Answer:** The standard model used is the **Pinhole Camera Model**.

**Q. 11. Does perspective projection preserve the parallelism of 3D lines in a 2D image?**
**Main Answer:** **No**, parallel lines in 3D space generally do not remain parallel in the 2D projected image; they appear to converge at a "vanishing point" (unless they are parallel to the image plane).

**Q. 12. What physical property of an image does Sampling digitize?**
**Main Answer:** **Sampling** digitizes the continuous **spatial coordinates** $(x,y)$ into a discrete grid of pixels.

**Q. 13. What physical property of an image does Quantization digitize?**
**Main Answer:** **Quantization** digitizes the continuous **amplitude** (brightness or intensity values) $f(x,y)$ into discrete integer levels.

**Q. 14. What is Uniform Quantization?**
**Main Answer:** **Uniform Quantization** occurs when the continuous range of image intensity is divided into discrete gray-level intervals of **equal step size**.

**Q. 15. What is Non-uniform Quantization?**
**Main Answer:** **Non-uniform Quantization** utilizes **variable step sizes** for gray levels, concentrating finer quantization levels in specific intensity ranges (like dark areas) where the eye is more sensitive.

**Q. 16. Name one reason why non-uniform quantization might be preferred over uniform quantization.**
**Main Answer:** It is preferred because the **Human Visual System (Weber's Law)** is more sensitive to small changes in darker regions than in brighter regions; non-uniform quantization optimizes bit usage based on human perception.

**Q. 17. What is Aliasing in the context of image sampling?**
**Main Answer:** **Aliasing** is a visual artifact (like Moiré patterns or jagged edges) that occurs when an image is under-sampled below the **Nyquist rate**, causing high-frequency details to masquerade as low-frequency patterns.

**Q. 1. Explain the Simple Image Formation Model. Discuss the nature and typical values of the illumination and reflectance components with real-world examples.**
**Introduction:**
A digital image is mathematically modeled as a continuous 2D function $f(x,y)$. The intensity value at any spatial coordinate is fundamentally generated by the interaction of light with an object.

**Main Answer:**
*   **The Model:** The simple image formation model states that image intensity is the product of two components:
    $$f(x,y) = i(x,y) \cdot r(x,y)$$
*   **Illumination Component $i(x,y)$:**
    *   Represents the amount of source light incident on the scene being viewed.
    *   It is determined strictly by the light source and has a range of $0 < i(x,y) < \infty$.
    *   *Real-world Example:* A clear sunny day produces $\approx 90,000$ foot-candles (fc) of illumination, whereas a standard indoor commercial office produces only $\approx 100$ fc.
*   **Reflectance Component $r(x,y)$:**
    *   Represents the amount of illumination reflected by the objects in the scene.
    *   It is an intrinsic property of the object's material, bounded by total absorption (0) and total reflectance (1): $0 < r(x,y) < 1$.
    *   *Real-world Example:* Black velvet has a reflectance of $\approx 0.01$, while flat white wall paint has a reflectance of $\approx 0.80$, and fresh snow approaches $\approx 0.93$.

**Conclusion:**
In this model, the overall dynamic range of a captured image is predominantly governed by the extreme variability of the illumination $i(x,y)$, rather than the bounded reflectance $r(x,y)$.

**Q. 2. What are Geometric Transformations in digital image processing? Represent Translation and Scaling using 3x3 homogeneous transformation matrices.**
**Introduction:**
**Geometric Transformations** modify the spatial relationships between pixels in an image. They are used to resize, shift, rotate, or correct physical distortions in an image without altering the actual pixel color values.

**Main Answer:**
To avoid the mathematical complexity of mixing additions (for translation) and multiplications (for scaling/rotation), we use **Homogeneous Coordinates**, which add a third dummy coordinate $(x, y, 1)$ allowing all operations to be treated as matrix multiplications.

1.  **Translation Matrix:** 
    Moves a pixel by shifting its coordinates by $t_x$ along the X-axis and $t_y$ along the Y-axis.
    $$ \begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
2.  **Scaling Matrix:**
    Changes the size of an image. If $S_x, S_y > 1$, the image enlarges. If $0 < S_x, S_y < 1$, it shrinks.
    $$ \begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} S_x & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & S_y & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Conclusion:**
By representing these transformations as $3 \times 3$ matrices, complex graphical operations can be rapidly executed using linear algebra hardware inside modern computers.

**Q. 3. Derive the transformation matrix for rotating a pixel coordinate $(x,y)$ by an angle $\theta$ in the counter-clockwise direction about the origin.**
**Introduction:**
To derive the 2D rotation matrix, we convert the Cartesian coordinates of the pixel into Polar coordinates, apply the angular rotation, and then convert back to Cartesian coordinates.

**Main Answer:**
1.  **Polar Representation:** Let the initial point $P(x,y)$ be at a distance $r$ from the origin, at an angle $\phi$ to the X-axis.
    *   $x = r \cos\phi$
    *   $y = r \sin\phi$
2.  **Apply Rotation:** We rotate the point by an angle $\theta$ to reach the new point $P'(x', y')$. The new angle from the X-axis is $(\phi + \theta)$.
    *   $x' = r \cos(\phi + \theta)$
    *   $y' = r \sin(\phi + \theta)$
3.  **Trigonometric Expansion:** Using angle sum formulas:
    *   $x' = r (\cos\phi \cos\theta - \sin\phi \sin\theta)$
    *   $y' = r (\sin\phi \cos\theta + \cos\phi \sin\theta)$
4.  **Substitution:** Substitute the original $x$ and $y$ back into the expanded equations:
    *   $x' = x \cos\theta - y \sin\theta$
    *   $y' = y \cos\theta + x \sin\theta$
5.  **Matrix Form:** Representing this as a column vector multiplication:
    $$ \begin{bmatrix} x' \\ y' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} $$

**Conclusion:**
This derived matrix forms the fundamental basis for all rotation algorithms in image processing and computer graphics.

**Q. 4. Explain how multiple geometric transformations (e.g., translation followed by rotation) can be combined. Why is matrix multiplication order important here?**
**Introduction:**
In practical image processing, an image rarely undergoes just one transformation. It usually experiences a sequence of operations (e.g., center, scale, then rotate). Combining these is known as **Composite Transformation**.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Combining Transformations:** Instead of applying matrices to millions of pixels one by one for each step, we multiply the transformation matrices together *first* to create a single "Composite Matrix". We then apply this single matrix to the pixels.
    *Composite Matrix $M = M_3 \cdot M_2 \cdot M_1$*
*   **The Importance of Order:** Matrix multiplication is **non-commutative** ($A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$). The order in which transformations are applied drastically changes the final position of the pixel.
    *   *Example:* Suppose you have a pixel at $(10, 0)$. 
    *   If you **Translate by 5** on X, then **Rotate $90^\circ$**: New point is at $(15, 0)$, rotation moves it to $(0, 15)$.
    *   If you **Rotate $90^\circ$** first, then **Translate by 5** on X: Rotation moves $(10, 0)$ to $(0, 10)$. Translation moves it to $(5, 10)$.
*   **Mathematical Representation:** For a point column vector $P$, transformations are applied right-to-left: $P' = (R \cdot T) \cdot P$.

**Conclusion:**
Misordering matrices is a common source of catastrophic errors in geometric modeling. Transformations must always be written in the exact reverse order of their intended sequential application.

**Q. 5. Explain the concept of Perspective Projection with the help of a basic pinhole camera model diagram.**
**Introduction:**
**Perspective projection** is the mathematical and optical process of projecting a 3D physical scene onto a 2D imaging plane. It mimics how human eyes and standard cameras perceive the world.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Concept:** In perspective projection, light rays originating from 3D objects travel in straight lines and converge at a single central point (Center of Projection). The image plane intercepts these rays, forming the 2D image. As a result, objects further away appear smaller, and parallel lines converge at a vanishing point.
*   **Pinhole Camera Diagram:**
```text
          (X,Y,Z) [3D Object]
             * 
              \
               \
                \     [Image Plane]
                 \          |
                  \         * (x,y) [Projected 2D Image]
                   \        |
====================*======================= (Optical Axis / Z-axis)
      [Center of Projection / Pinhole (0,0,0)]
                    |       |
                    |<--λ-->|
                  (Focal Length)
```
*   **Mechanism:** The pinhole restricts light so that only one ray from a specific point on the 3D object strikes a specific point on the 2D image sensor (located at a focal length $\lambda$ from the pinhole).

**Conclusion:**
This model forms the core geometry for computer vision, defining how scale and depth inherently collapse during 2D photograph formation.

**Q. 6. Explain the mathematical mapping of 3D world coordinates $(X,Y,Z)$ to 2D image plane coordinates $(x,y)$ under perspective projection.**
**Introduction:**
To compute exactly where a 3D point will land on a 2D camera sensor, we use the geometric property of similar triangles derived from the pinhole camera model.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Geometry Setup:** Let the center of projection (the pinhole) be at the origin $(0,0,0)$. The 3D object point is at $(X,Y,Z)$. The image plane is positioned at a distance $\lambda$ (focal length) along the Z-axis, meaning the plane is at $z = \lambda$.
*   **Derivation via Similar Triangles:**
    Looking at the geometry along the X-Z plane, a triangle is formed from the origin to $(X,Z)$, and a smaller similar triangle is formed from the origin to the image plane at $(x, \lambda)$.
    Because the triangles are similar, the ratio of their heights to their bases must be equal:
    $$ \frac{x}{\lambda} = \frac{X}{Z} \implies x = \frac{\lambda \cdot X}{Z} $$
    Similarly, looking at the Y-Z plane:
    $$ \frac{y}{\lambda} = \frac{Y}{Z} \implies y = \frac{\lambda \cdot Y}{Z} $$
*   *(Note: If the image plane is placed behind the pinhole at $-\lambda$ as in physical cameras, the formulas result in $x = \frac{-\lambda X}{Z}$ and $y = \frac{-\lambda Y}{Z}$, mathematically indicating that the image is formed upside down).*

**Conclusion:**
These equations prove that the 2D coordinates $(x,y)$ are inversely proportional to the depth $Z$. As depth $Z$ increases, $x$ and $y$ shrink, creating the hallmark optical illusion of perspective.

**Q. 7. Differentiate between Sampling and Quantization. How do these two steps determine the spatial and gray-level resolution of a digital image?**
**Introduction:**
A continuous optical image is analog in both spatial positioning and brightness. To digitize it for computer processing, two distinct discretization steps are required: Sampling and Quantization.

**Main Answer:**
| Feature | Sampling | Quantization |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | Digitizing the spatial coordinates $(x,y)$ of the image. | Digitizing the amplitude/intensity $f(x,y)$ of each pixel. |

**Conclusion:**
Sampling defines the grid size of the matrix, while Quantization restricts the number values that can be stored inside each cell of that matrix. Both must be sufficiently high to create a perceptually flawless digital replica.

**Q. 8. Differentiate between Uniform and Non-uniform sampling. Under what conditions is non-uniform sampling highly beneficial?**
**Introduction:**
Sampling involves superimposing a grid over an image to extract pixel points. The spacing of this grid can either be fixed or adaptively varied.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Uniform Sampling:** 
    The spatial grid has a fixed, constant distance between every pixel in both horizontal and vertical directions. It is universally used in standard consumer cameras.
*   **Non-uniform (Adaptive) Sampling:** 
    The sampling grid has variable spacing. It places a highly dense grid of pixels in regions of the image with rapid intensity changes (sharp details, edges) and a very sparse grid in regions with constant background intensity.

**Conditions for Benefit:**
Non-uniform sampling is highly beneficial when processing images characterized by vast, empty backgrounds with tiny regions of intricate detail (e.g., satellite imagery of stars, or medical scans of isolated tumors).
*   *Advantage:* It drastically reduces the overall file size and storage requirements by not wasting pixels on empty, featureless space, while perfectly preserving high-frequency critical details.

**Conclusion:**
While mathematically elegant for compression, non-uniform sampling is hardware-intensive to capture directly, usually making it a post-processing algorithm rather than a primary acquisition method.

**Q. 9. Explain Uniform vs. Non-uniform Quantization. Briefly explain how the human visual system (Weber's ratio) justifies the use of non-uniform quantization.**
**Introduction:**
Quantization divides the continuous intensity range into discrete digital steps. The spacing of these steps heavily influences visual quality.

**Main Answer:**
*   **Uniform Quantization:** The intensity range (e.g., 0 to 255) is divided into completely equal intervals. The step size is constant regardless of whether the area is pitch black or blindingly white.
*   **Non-uniform Quantization:** The intensity range is divided into unequal intervals. The step sizes are dynamically assigned based on the probability density of the pixel values or the characteristics of human vision.
*   **Justification using Weber's Ratio:**
    The Human Visual System is logarithmic, not linear. **Weber's Law** states that the human eye is extremely sensitive to tiny changes in brightness in **dark regions**, but is "blind" to those exact same tiny changes in **bright regions**. 
    Therefore, Non-uniform Quantization assigns very fine, narrow quantization steps to darker intensity levels (preventing dark banding), and assigns wide, coarse steps to bright intensity levels (saving bits where the eye won't notice the difference anyway).

**Conclusion:**
By tailoring the quantization steps to the biological flaws of the human eye, non-uniform quantization achieves perceptually superior image quality using significantly fewer bits per pixel.

### Question 1: Focus on Geometric Transformations (7 + 8)

**(a) What is the need for homogeneous coordinates in geometric modeling? Write down the $3 \times 3$ homogeneous transformation matrices for Translation, Uniform Scaling, and Rotation. (7)**

**Main Answer:**
**Need for Homogeneous Coordinates:**
In standard Cartesian coordinates $(x,y)$, rotation and scaling are achieved via matrix multiplication, but translation is achieved via matrix *addition*.
To apply a sequence of transformations, a computer would have to constantly alternate between multiplication and addition, preventing the creation of a single, efficient composite matrix. 
**Homogeneous coordinates** solve this by adding a dummy third coordinate $W$ (usually 1), transforming 2D points into 3D vectors $(x, y, 1)$. This elevates translation from an addition to a matrix multiplication. Thus, all geometric transformations become uniform multiplications, allowing modern graphics hardware to combine hundreds of transformations into one single $3 \times 3$ matrix before applying it to the image matrix.

**Homogeneous Transformation Matrices:**
1.  **Translation Matrix $T(t_x, t_y)$:**
    $$ T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
2.  **Uniform Scaling Matrix $S(s)$:** (Where $S_x = S_y = s$)
    $$ S = \begin{bmatrix} s & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & s & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
3.  **Rotation Matrix $R(\theta)$:** (Counter-clockwise rotation about the origin)
    $$ R = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**(b) A pixel at coordinate $(4, 4)$ is subjected to the following sequence of transformations: (i) Translation by $t_x=2, t_y=3$. (ii) Scaling by $S_x=2, S_y=2$. (iii) Rotation by $90^\circ$ counter-clockwise. Find the final coordinates of the pixel using matrix multiplication. (8)**

**Main Answer:**
**Given:**
Initial Point $P = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$
The transformations must be applied sequentially: First Translation ($T$), then Scaling ($S$), then Rotation ($R$).
Therefore, the final coordinate $P'$ is calculated as: $P' = R \cdot S \cdot T \cdot P$

**Step 1: Apply Translation ($T$)**
$T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$P_1 = T \cdot P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (1)(4) + (0)(4) + (2)(1) \\ (0)(4) + (1)(4) + (3)(1) \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 7 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$
*(Intermediate Point after Translation: $x=6, y=7$)*

**Step 2: Apply Scaling ($S$)**
$S = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$P_2 = S \cdot P_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 7 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 14 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$
*(Intermediate Point after Scaling: $x=12, y=14$)*

**Step 3: Apply Rotation ($R$)**
$\theta = 90^\circ$. We know $\cos(90^\circ) = 0$ and $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.
$R = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
$P_{final} = R \cdot P_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ 14 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (0)(12) + (-1)(14) + 0 \\ (1)(12) + (0)(14) + 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -14 \\ 12 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

**Conclusion:**
Using matrix multiplication, the final transformed coordinates of the pixel are **$(-14, 12)$**.

**(c) What is the inverse perspective projection problem? Why is it impossible to perfectly reconstruct a 3D scene from a single 2D perspective projection? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
The **Inverse Perspective Projection Problem** is the challenge of attempting to calculate the original 3D world coordinates $(X,Y,Z)$ of an object using only the 2D coordinates $(x,y)$ captured in a photograph.

**Why it is impossible:**
The perspective projection equations are $x = \frac{\lambda X}{Z}$ and $y = \frac{\lambda Y}{Z}$.
During projection, the 3D depth dimension ($Z$) is completely collapsed and lost into the 2D plane. When trying to invert this mapping, we have 2 equations but 3 unknown variables ($X, Y, Z$). Mathematically, this system has infinite solutions. 
Physically, this means a small toy car placed close to the camera lens creates the exact same 2D projected image as a massive real car placed miles away. Without stereoscopic vision (two cameras) or range-finding sensors to estimate $Z$, perfect 3D reconstruction from a single 2D image is fundamentally impossible.

### Question 3: Focus on Sampling, Quantization & Digitization (7 + 4 + 4)

**(a) Detail the processes of Sampling and Quantization in converting a continuous-tone image into a digital image. How do these relate to the size and quality of the image matrix? (7)**

**Main Answer:**
A continuous-tone optical image contains infinite spatial points and infinite color variations. Digitization requires slicing both of these infinite domains into finite grids.

**1. Sampling (Spatial Digitization):**
*   *Process:* A grid is superimposed over the continuous image $f(x,y)$. The continuous spatial coordinates are discretized into an $M \times N$ matrix of points (pixels). 
*   *Relating to Size/Quality:* Sampling dictates the **Spatial Resolution**. A high sampling rate (e.g., $4000 \times 3000$ pixels) produces a sharp, high-quality image capable of zooming without blurring, but generates a massive matrix size requiring heavy storage. A low sampling rate causes "Pixelation" or "Checkerboarding".

**2. Quantization (Amplitude Digitization):**
*   *Process:* The continuous voltage or brightness value at each sampled pixel is rounded to the nearest discrete integer level. The number of allowed levels is defined by $L = 2^k$ (where $k$ is the bit depth).
*   *Relating to Size/Quality:* Quantization dictates the **Gray-level Resolution**. An 8-bit image ($k=8$) yields 256 smooth shades of gray. High quantization ensures smooth, photo-realistic color gradients but increases the number of bits stored per pixel ($M \times N \times k$ bits). Low quantization causes abrupt color banding.

**(b) Compare Uniform and Non-uniform quantization. Discuss the "Lloyd-Max quantizer" concept or how finer quantization steps are assigned in non-uniform quantization. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
*   **Uniform Quantization:** Assigns identical, equally spaced step sizes between all gray levels, from pure black to pure white. It is simple to implement but visually inefficient.
*   **Non-uniform Quantization:** Assigns variable step sizes. 
*   **Lloyd-Max Quantizer Concept:** Non-uniform quantization utilizes mathematical algorithms like the Lloyd-Max quantizer, which studies the Probability Density Function (PDF) of the image's histogram. 
    It minimizes the Mean Square Error (MSE) by placing **dense, fine quantization steps** in intensity ranges that occur frequently in the image, and **sparse, coarse steps** in intensity ranges that are rare. Additionally, based on human visual biology, it assigns finer steps in dark regions (where eyes detect noise easily) and coarser steps in bright regions.

**(c) Explain the phenomenon of 'False Contouring'. Is it caused by improper sampling or improper quantization? (4)**

**Main Answer:**
**False Contouring** is a visual degradation artifact where smooth, continuous color gradients (such as a clear blue sky or human skin tone) break down into sudden, artificial, and highly visible banding that resembles a topographic contour map.

**Cause:**
False contouring is caused exclusively by **improper (insufficient) Quantization**, not sampling. If an image is digitized using a very low bit depth (e.g., 3 bits, yielding only 8 possible gray levels), there are simply not enough discrete numerical levels to represent the subtle, smooth transition of light in the physical scene. The system is forced to aggressively round nearby colors into the same single bucket, creating harsh artificial boundaries between color zones.

### Question 4: Mixed Theoretical & Mathematical Bag (5 + 5 + 5)

**(a) Derive the transformation matrix to rotate an image by an angle $\theta$ around an arbitrary point $(x_0, y_0)$ instead of the origin. (Hint: Translate to origin -> Rotate -> Translate back). (5)**

**Main Answer:**
To rotate an object around an arbitrary pivot point $(x_0, y_0)$, we cannot simply apply the standard rotation matrix, as that specifically rotates the entire space around the $(0,0)$ origin. We must use a composite transformation.

**Steps:**
1.  **Translate to Origin:** Shift the image so that the pivot point $(x_0, y_0)$ rests on the origin $(0,0)$.
    $$ T_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -x_0 \\ 0 & 1 & -y_0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
2.  **Rotate:** Apply the standard rotation by $\theta$ around the origin.
    $$ R = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$
3.  **Translate Back:** Shift the image back to the original pivot point location.
    $$ T_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & x_0 \\ 0 & 1 & y_0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Composite Matrix Derivation:**
$$ M_{composite} = T_2 \cdot R \cdot T_1 $$
*(Note: As required by matrix multiplication properties, the operations are ordered from right to left).*
By multiplying these three $3 \times 3$ matrices together, we generate the final, unified matrix required to rotate an object flawlessly around any arbitrary center.

**(b) Discuss the effect of varying the sampling rate on an image. What happens if the sampling rate is below the Nyquist rate? (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Effect of Varying Sampling Rate:**
The sampling rate dictates the dimensions of the pixel grid. 
*   *Increasing the rate:* Captures higher spatial frequencies (finer details, sharper edges), increasing image clarity at the cost of higher storage requirements.
*   *Decreasing the rate:* Discards high-frequency detail, resulting in a blocky, pixelated image (the Checkerboard effect).

**Below the Nyquist Rate (Aliasing):**
According to the Shannon-Nyquist theorem, to accurately capture an optical signal, the sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency present in the image.
If the sampling rate drops **below the Nyquist rate**, a catastrophic artifact known as **Aliasing** occurs. The high-frequency details (like fine stripes on a shirt or brick patterns on a building) are mathematically misidentified by the digital grid. They "alias" themselves, manifesting as fake, low-frequency wave patterns known as **Moiré patterns**, permanently corrupting the image structure.

**(c) An image is represented by $f(x,y) = i(x,y) \cdot r(x,y)$. State whether $i(x,y)$ or $r(x,y)$ is responsible for the overall dynamic range of the image, and briefly explain why. (5)**

**Main Answer:**
The **Illumination component $i(x,y)$** is overwhelmingly responsible for the overall dynamic range of the image.

**Explanation:**
Dynamic range is the ratio between the maximum and minimum measurable light intensities in an image. 
*   The reflectance $r(x,y)$ of physical objects is strictly bounded between 0 and 1. Even the starkest contrast in materials—from black velvet ($r=0.01$) to polished silver ($r=0.99$)—provides a dynamic range of barely $100:1$.
*   Conversely, the illumination $i(x,y)$ varies wildly. Starlight generates about $0.0001$ lux, while direct sunlight generates over $100,000$ lux. 
Therefore, because illumination can span multiple orders of magnitude ($1,000,000,000:1$), it completely dictates and dominates the final dynamic range constraints of the captured image $f(x,y)$.

### Question 5: Focus on Non-uniformity & Geometric Concepts (6 + 5 + 4)

**(a) Justify the statement: "Non-uniform sampling is best suited for images where details are concentrated in certain regions, while non-uniform quantization is best suited to match human eye perception." (6)**

**Main Answer:**
**Justifying Non-uniform Sampling:**
In images where 90% of the area is a flat, uniform background (like a clear sky) and 10% is highly detailed (like a flock of birds), standard uniform sampling wastes millions of pixels measuring empty space. Non-uniform sampling adaptively places a highly dense pixel grid over the birds (preserving fine edges) and a sparse grid over the sky. This optimizes data storage and processing power without losing structural clarity in the areas that matter.

**Justifying Non-uniform Quantization:**
The human eye does not perceive changes in brightness linearly. According to Weber's Law, our eyes are highly sensitive to "noise" and subtle gradient shifts in dark shadows, but we are effectively blind to small intensity changes in blindingly bright areas. Uniform quantization wastes bits by dividing the bright areas into tiny steps the eye cannot even see. Non-uniform quantization solves this by dedicating fine, narrow numerical steps to dark regions (eliminating false contouring where it is most noticeable) and wide, coarse steps to bright regions, perfectly matching biological perception.

**(b) Define the vanishing point in Perspective Projection. Why do parallel lines in a 3D scene appear to converge in a 2D photograph? (5)**

**Main Answer:**
**Vanishing Point Definition:**
In perspective projection, a **vanishing point** is the specific 2D coordinate on the image plane where a set of mutually parallel 3D lines appear to intersect as they recede into the distance. 

**Why Convergence Occurs:**
This is an optical illusion dictated by the mathematics of the pinhole model: $x = \frac{\lambda X}{Z}$.
As the 3D depth dimension ($Z$) approaches infinity ($\infty$), the physical $(X,Y)$ spread of parallel lines is divided by an increasingly massive number. 
Mathematically, $\lim_{Z \to \infty} \frac{\lambda X}{Z} = 0$. 
This means that regardless of how far apart two parallel train tracks are in the 3D world (the $X$ separation), as they recede into the extreme distance (massive $Z$), their 2D projected coordinates $(x)$ are squeezed together closer and closer to the optical axis, until they visually merge at the vanishing point.

**(c) Show that two successive translations $T_1(t_{x1}, t_{y1})$ and $T_2(t_{x2}, t_{y2})$ are additive in nature using matrix representation. (4)**

**Main Answer:**
To prove that successive translations are additive, we multiply their homogeneous transformation matrices together.

Let $T_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_{x1} \\ 0 & 1 & t_{y1} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ and $T_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_{x2} \\ 0 & 1 & t_{y2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

Applying $T_1$ followed by $T_2$ creates the composite matrix $T_{composite} = T_2 \cdot T_1$:
$$ T_{composite} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_{x2} \\ 0 & 1 & t_{y2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_{x1} \\ 0 & 1 & t_{y1} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Performing row-by-column matrix multiplication:
*   Row 1: $(1)(1)+0+0, \quad 0+(0)(1)+0, \quad (1)(t_{x1}) + (0)(t_{y1}) + (t_{x2})(1) = \mathbf{t_{x1} + t_{x2}}$
*   Row 2: $0+0+0, \quad 0+(1)(1)+0, \quad (0)(t_{x1}) + (1)(t_{y1}) + (t_{y2})(1) = \mathbf{t_{y1} + t_{y2}}$
*   Row 3: $0, \quad 0, \quad 1$

Resulting Matrix:
$$ T_{composite} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_{x1} + t_{x2} \\ 0 & 1 & t_{y1} + t_{y2} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Conclusion:**
The multiplication of the two matrices mathematically results in a single matrix where the translation factors are simply added together, definitively proving the additive nature of successive translations.
